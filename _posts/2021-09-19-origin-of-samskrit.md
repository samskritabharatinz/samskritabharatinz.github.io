Here is a little introduction to the history and origin of Samskrit. It belongs to the Indo-European family of languages. It is one of the three earliest ancient documented languages that arose from a common root language now referred to as the Proto-Indo-European language.

As mentioned in our previous post, we will use the words Sanskrit and Samskrit interchangeably.

## Vedic Samskrit

The pre-classical form of the language is Vedic Sanskrit. The earliest attested Sanskrit text is the Rigveda, a Hindu scripture from the mid- to late-second millennium BCE. Vedic Sanskrit was both a spoken and literary language of ancient India. According to Michael Witzel, Vedic Sanskrit was a spoken language of the semi-nomadic Aryans who temporarily settled in one place, maintained cattle herds, practised limited agriculture, and after some time moved by wagon trains they called *grama*.

The Vedic Sanskrit language, or a closely related Indo-European variant, was recognized beyond ancient India, as evidenced by the "Mitanni Treaty" between the ancient Hittite and Mitanni people, carved into a rock, in a region that is now parts of Syria and Turkey. Parts of this treaty, such as the names of the Mitanni princes and technical terms related to horse training, are, for reasons not understood, in early forms of Vedic Sanskrit. The treaty also invokes the gods Varuna, Mitra, Indra, and Nasatya, found in the earliest layers of Vedic literature.

## Classical Samskrit

The formalization of the Sanskrit language is credited to *Pāṇini*, along with Patanjali's *Mahābhāṣya* and Katyayana's commentary that preceded Patanjali's work. Panini composed the *Aṣṭādhyāyī* ('Eight-Chapter Grammar'). His work is generally accepted to be from sometime between the 6th and 4th centuries BCE.
